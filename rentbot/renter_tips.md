# Renter Tips & Advice

## Lease

Before signing a lease, read every word — it is a legally binding contract.
Key things to look for:

- **Lease length**: Most are 12 months. Month-to-month leases offer flexibility but cost more.
- **Rent increase clauses**: Some leases allow the landlord to raise rent mid-lease. Watch for this.
- **Early termination fee**: Know the penalty if you need to break the lease early. Usually 1-2 months' rent.
- **Renewal terms**: Does the lease auto-renew? What notice do you need to give to move out?
- **Guest policies**: Some leases restrict how long guests can stay.
- **Pet clauses**: If you have or plan to get a pet, confirm it's allowed and understand any pet fees.
- **Subletting rules**: Check if you're allowed to sublet if your situation changes.

Always get any verbal promises from your landlord in writing before signing.

## Deposit

Security deposits protect the landlord against damage or unpaid rent.

- **Typical amount**: 1-2 months' rent, though this varies by state.
- **Document everything**: Before moving in, take dated photos and video of every room. Email them to your landlord to create a paper trail.
- **Get a move-in checklist**: Many landlords provide one — fill it out honestly and keep a copy.
- **Know your state's laws**: Most states require landlords to return deposits within 14-30 days of move-out.
- **Deductions**: Landlords can only deduct for damage beyond normal "wear and tear." Nail holes and minor scuffs are usually fine; large stains and broken fixtures are not.
- **Interest**: Some states require landlords to keep deposits in a separate account and pay you interest.

## Utilities

Clarify which utilities are included in rent before signing.

- **Common included utilities**: Water, trash, sometimes heat or internet.
- **Common tenant-paid utilities**: Electricity, gas, internet, renter's insurance.
- **Average monthly costs**: Budget roughly $100-200/mo for electricity, $50-80 for gas, $50-80 for internet.
- **Set up accounts early**: Contact utility companies a week before move-in so service is active on day one.
- **Energy efficiency**: Ask about the age of the HVAC system and appliances — older units drive up electric bills.
- **Renter's insurance**: Not always required but highly recommended. Typically costs $15-30/month and covers theft, fire, and liability.

## Moving

Planning your move carefully saves time and money.

- **Give notice**: Most leases require 30-60 days written notice before moving out.
- **Book movers early**: Professional movers fill up fast, especially on weekends and at month-end.
- **DIY vs. movers**: A rental truck (U-Haul, Penske) costs $100-400 for local moves. Full-service movers run $800-2,500+.
- **Change your address**: Update USPS, your bank, employer, subscriptions, and government IDs.
- **Transfer or cancel utilities**: Schedule disconnection at your old place and connection at the new one.
- **Clean the old unit**: Leave the apartment in the same condition you found it to maximize your deposit return.
- **Return all keys**: Keep a record (email confirmation or receipt) that keys were returned.

## Rights

Knowing your rights as a renter protects you from bad landlords.

- **Habitability**: Landlords must keep your unit safe and livable — working heat, plumbing, no mold or pest infestations.
- **Entry notice**: In most states, landlords must give 24-48 hours' notice before entering your unit (except emergencies).
- **Anti-discrimination**: The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability, or familial status.
- **Retaliation protection**: A landlord cannot legally evict you or raise your rent because you complained about conditions.
- **Eviction process**: Landlords must follow a legal process to evict you — they cannot change locks or remove your belongings without a court order.
- **Rent control**: Some cities have rent control ordinances that limit how much rent can increase. Check local laws.
- **Resources**: If you believe your rights have been violated, contact your local tenant's rights organization or legal aid.
